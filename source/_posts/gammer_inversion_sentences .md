## Knowledge Points Part

**What is Inversion?**
In English, the standard word order is **Subject + Verb** (e.g., *She is*). Inversion means reversing this order to **Verb + Subject** (e.g., *Is she*?).

We use inversion in two main ways:
1.  **Question Formation** (Grammatical necessity).
2.  **Emphatic/Stylistic Inversion** (To make a sentence more dramatic or formal).

---

### A. Inversion in Questions
This is the most common type.
- **Yes/No Questions:** Auxiliary verb + Subject + Main verb.
  - *Are* you coming?
  - *Has* she finished?
- **WH- Questions:** WH-word + Auxiliary + Subject + Main verb.
  - *Where* did you go?
  - *Why* is he crying?

---

### B. Inversion with Negative Adverbials (Formal)
When a sentence begins with a negative or restrictive word, we invert the subject and the auxiliary verb.

| Adverbial | Meaning/Usage | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Never** | At no time | *Never* have I seen such a beautiful sunset. |
| **Seldom / Rarely** | Almost never | *Rarely* does he arrive on time. |
| **Hardly / Scarcely... when** | Almost immediately | *Hardly* had I sat down when the phone rang. |
| **No sooner... than** | Immediately after | *No sooner* had we left than it started to rain. |
| **Not only... but also** | Adds emphasis | *Not only* is she a doctor, *but* she *also* writes novels. |
| **At no time** | Never | *At no time* did he admit he was wrong. |
| **Under no circumstances** | Definitely not | *Under no circumstances* should you open that door. |
| **In no way** | Not at all | *In no way* was he responsible for the accident. |

---

### C. Inversion with "Only"
When "only" + (time phrase, preposition, or clause) is placed at the start for emphasis.

| Structure | Example |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Only + time** (now, then, later) | *Only now* do I understand the problem. |
| **Only + prepositional phrase** (by, in, with) | *Only by working hard* will you pass the exam. |
| **Only + after/before/when + clause** | *Only after* I had eaten did I feel better. |

---

### D. Inversion with "So + Adjective" and "Such"
Used for dramatic emphasis in formal writing.

- **So + Adjective + Auxiliary + Subject + Verb**  
  *So* talented *is* she that she plays three instruments.  
  *So* cold *was* it that the water froze.

- **Such + Be + Subject + That clause**  
  *Such* was the noise *that* I couldn't sleep.  
  *Such* were his lies *that* no one trusted him.

---

### E. Inversion in Conditional Sentences (Formal)
In formal English, you can remove "if" and invert the auxiliary verb.

| Original Condition | Inverted Version (Formal) |
| :--- | :--- |
| If I **were** rich... | **Were** I rich... |
| If he **had** known... | **Had** he known... |
| If she **should** call... | **Should** she call... |

---

### F. Inversion with "Here" and "There" (Place Adverbials)
When a place adverbial (here, there, inside, out) starts the sentence AND the subject is a noun (not a pronoun).

- **Correct:** *Here* comes the bus. (Bus = noun)
- **Not Inverted:** *Here* it comes. (It = pronoun, no inversion)
