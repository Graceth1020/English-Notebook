## PAY

| Phrasal Verb | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **pay back** | 1. To return borrowed money. <br> 2. To take revenge or retaliate. | 1. I promise to **pay you back** next week. <br> 2. He cheated me—I'll **pay him back** someday. |
| **pay off** | 1. To settle a debt in full. <br> 2. To be worthwhile or result in success. <br> 3. To bribe someone. | 1. It took 10 years to **pay off** the mortgage. <br> 2. All that hard work finally **paid off** when I got the promotion. <br> 3. They **paid off** the guard to let them in. |
| **pay for** | 1. To bear the cost of something. <br> 2. To suffer consequences for a mistake or wrongdoing. | 1. Who's going to **pay for** dinner? <br> 2. He'll **pay for** his mistakes. |
| **pay up** | To pay what is owed (often reluctantly or after pressure). | The deadline has passed—you need to **pay up** now! |
| **pay out** | 1. To spend or disburse money (often a large sum). <br> 2. To release rope or line gradually. | 1. The insurance company **paid out** a large sum. <br> 2. The fisherman **paid out** the line slowly. |
| **pay into** | To contribute money regularly to an account, fund, or scheme. | I **pay into** my pension plan every month. |
| **pay down** | To reduce a debt by making partial payments (especially principal). | We're trying to **pay down** our credit card debt. |

## Final Note on Phrasal Verbs

- **Transitive vs. Intransitive:** Some phrasal verbs take a direct object (*put off the meeting*), while others do not (*get up*).
- **Separable vs. Inseparable:** With many transitive phrasal verbs, you can put the object between the verb and the particle (*take your shoes off* = *take off your shoes*).
- **Context is key:** Many phrasal verbs have multiple meanings depending on the situation—always pay attention to the context!
