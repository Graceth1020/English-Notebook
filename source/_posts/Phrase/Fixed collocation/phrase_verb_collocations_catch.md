## CATCH

| Phrasal Verb | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **catch up (with)** | To reach someone ahead of you; to bring yourself up to date | I ran to **catch up with** him. / Let's meet up and **catch up** on old times. |
| **catch on** | To become popular; to understand something | This new dance craze has really **caught on**. / It took me a while to **catch on**, but I finally got it. |
| **catch out** | To trap or expose someone's mistake or lie | The interviewer **caught him out** with a tricky question. |
| **catch up in** | To become involved in something (often unwillingly) | I don't want to get **caught up in** their political argument. |
| **catch off** | (Less common) – Usually used as "catch off guard" (not a phrasal verb, but a fixed phrase) | The question **caught me off guard** – I didn't know what to say. |
| **catch at** | To try to grasp or seize (sometimes figuratively) | She **caught at** his sleeve before he walked away. |

## Final Note on Phrasal Verbs

- **Transitive vs. Intransitive:** Some phrasal verbs take a direct object (*put off the meeting*), while others do not (*get up*).
- **Separable vs. Inseparable:** With many transitive phrasal verbs, you can put the object between the verb and the particle (*take your shoes off* = *take off your shoes*).
- **Context is key:** Many phrasal verbs have multiple meanings depending on the situation—always pay attention to the context!
