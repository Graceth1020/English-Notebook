## LOOK

| Phrasal Verb | Definition | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **look after** | 1. To take care of someone or something. <br> 2. To be responsible for something. | 1. Can you **look after** my cat while I'm away? <br> 2. She **looks after** the company's finances. |
| **look for** | 1. To search or seek. <br> 2. To expect or anticipate. | 1. I've been **looking for** my keys everywhere. <br> 2. We're **looking for** a reply from the client. |
| **look forward to** | To anticipate with pleasure (always followed by a noun or gerund). | I really **look forward to** the weekend. / I **look forward to** seeing you. |
| **look up** | 1. To search for information (in a book, database, or online). <br> 2. To improve (usually for a situation). <br> 3. To raise one's eyes. | 1. You can **look up** the word in the dictionary. <br> 2. Things are finally **looking up** for us. <br> 3. He **looked up** at the sky. |
| **look into** | To investigate, examine, or research something. | The police are **looking into** the robbery. |
| **look out** | 1. To be careful or watchful (used as a warning). <br> 2. To be alert to potential danger. | 1. **Look out!** There's a car coming! <br> 2. She always **looks out** for her younger brother. |
| **look over** | To review, examine, or inspect quickly. | Could you **look over** my essay before I submit it? |
| **look down on** | To view with contempt, disrespect, or superiority. | She **looks down on** people who don't have a degree. |
| **look up to** | To admire, respect, or regard with esteem. | I've always **looked up to** my older brother. |
| **look through** | 1. To browse or examine quickly (usually documents/items). <br> 2. To pretend not to see someone (ignore deliberately). | 1. She **looked through** the magazine while waiting. <br> 2. He just **looked through** me as if I didn't exist. |
| **look back on** | To remember, reflect on, or review the past. | I **look back on** my college days with fondness. |
| **look in on** | To visit briefly to check on someone or something. | I'll **look in on** Grandma on my way home. |

## Final Note on Phrasal Verbs

- **Transitive vs. Intransitive:** Some phrasal verbs take a direct object (*put off the meeting*), while others do not (*get up*).
- **Separable vs. Inseparable:** With many transitive phrasal verbs, you can put the object between the verb and the particle (*take your shoes off* = *take off your shoes*).
- **Context is key:** Many phrasal verbs have multiple meanings depending on the situation—always pay attention to the context!
