##  Knowledge Points Part

These two principles are primarily used to determine **Subject-Verb Agreement** (concord) when the subject is complex, contains multiple parts, or is separated from the verb.

---

### A. The Principle of Proximity (Nearness)

**Definition:**
The verb agrees with the **noun or pronoun closest to it** in the sentence, rather than with the main subject.

**When to use it:**

1. **With "Either... or" and "Neither... nor":**
  - The verb matches the subject that is nearest to it.
  - *Example:* Neither the teacher **nor** the students **are** here. (Plural because *students* is closest).
  - *Example:* Neither the students **nor** the teacher **is** here. (Singular because *teacher* is closest).

2. **With "Not only... but also":**
  - The verb matches the subject nearest to it.
  - *Example:* Not only the manager **but also** his assistants **have** been fired.
  - *Example:* Not only the assistants **but also** the manager **has** been fired.

3. **With "There is/are" (Existential sentences):**
  - In informal English, we often use "there is" + plural noun because the singular *is* comes right before the plural noun.
  - *Example:* There **is** a pen **and** three pencils on the desk. (Informal/Natural speech).
  - *(Note: Formal grammar prefers the Principle of Distance here, but Proximity is widely accepted in modern usage).*

---

### B. The Principle of Distance (Grammatical Concord / Notional Agreement)

**Definition:**
The verb agrees with the **head noun** (the main grammatical subject), ignoring all modifiers, prepositional phrases, and interrupting clauses that come *between* the subject and the verb. The verb "reaches across" the distance to match the core subject.

**When to use it:**

1. **With Prepositional Phrases (e.g., *of*, *in*, *with*, *along with*, *together with*, *as well as*):**
  - The main subject determines the verb. The phrase in between is just extra information.
  - *Example:* The **box** *of chocolates* **is** on the table. (Singular *box*).
  - *Example:* The **students** *along with their teacher* **are** going on a trip. (Plural *students*).

2. **With Relative Clauses (who, that, which):**
  - The verb inside the relative clause agrees with the **antecedent** (the noun the clause refers to), even if other words are nearby.
  - *Example:* She is one of those **people** *who* **never give** up. (Plural because *who* refers to *people*).
  - *Example:* She is the **only one** *of those people* *who* **never gives** up. (Singular because *who* refers to *one*).

3. **With "Each", "Every", and "Many a":**
  - Even if they are far from the verb, they force a singular verb.
  - *Example:* **Each** of the students **is** required to read the book.
  - *Example:* **Many a** soldier **has** died in battle.

4. **With Collective Nouns (e.g., *team, family, committee*):**
  - If the group acts as **one unit** → Singular verb (Distance/Concord).
  - If the members act **individually** → Plural verb (Proximity to the idea of individuals).
  - *Example (Unit):* The **team** *of players* **is** winning.
  - *Example (Individuals):* The **team** *are* arguing among themselves.
