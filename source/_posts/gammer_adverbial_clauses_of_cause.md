## The Main Conjunctions for Reason/Cause

| Conjunction | Formality | Position | Strength |
|-------------|-----------|----------|----------|
| **because** | Neutral | Before or after main clause | **Strongest** — direct cause |
| **since** | Formal | Usually before main clause | Medium — "given that" |
| **as** | Formal | Usually before main clause | Medium — "given that" |
| **now that** | Neutral | Usually before main clause | Medium — "because of a new situation" |
| **seeing that / seeing as** | Informal | Usually before main clause | Medium — "considering that" |
| **in that** | Very formal | Usually after main clause | Weak — explains *in what way* |
| **for** | Very formal / literary | **After** main clause, with comma | Weak — adds an explanation |

---

## Detailed Breakdown with Examples

### 1. **Because** — The Most Common and Strongest

**Use it when:** You want to give the **direct, most important reason**. It answers the question *"Why?"* with the clearest cause.

**Examples:**
- *I stayed home **because it was raining**.* (Why did you stay home? The rain is the direct cause.)
- *She passed **because she studied hard**.*

**Position:** Can come before or after the main clause.
- *Because it was raining, I stayed home.* (comma needed)
- *I stayed home because it was raining.* (no comma)

⚠️ **Only *because* can directly answer a *"Why?"* question:**
- *"Why are you late?" — "Because I missed the bus."* ✅
- *"Since I missed the bus."* ❌ (sounds unnatural)

⚠️ **Only *because* can be used with *just* or *only*:**
- *I left **just because** I was tired.* ✅
- *I left just since I was tired.* ❌

---

### 2. **Since** — "Given that" (Known Information)

**Use it when:** The reason is **already known** by the listener, or it's **background information**, not the main point. It's more formal than *because*.

**Examples:**
- **Since you're here**, let's start the meeting.
    - (I'm not explaining *why* you're here—we both know you're here. The main point is *let's start*.)
- *She didn't eat **since she wasn't hungry**.*
    - (Both know she wasn't hungry; the focus is on *not eating*.)

**Position:** Usually comes **before** the main clause (though it can follow).

**Key difference from *because*:**
- **Because** = "The reason is..." (new information, focus on the cause)
- **Since** = "Given that we both know..." (old information, focus on the result)

---

### 3. **As** — Very Similar to *Since*, More Formal

**Use it when:** The reason is **background information**, just like *since*. It's slightly more formal and often used in writing.

**Examples:**
- **As it was getting dark**, we turned on the lights.
    - (We both know it was getting dark—the main point is *turning on the lights*.)
- **As I was tired**, I went to bed early.

**Position:** Usually comes **before** the main clause.

**Difference between *as* and *since*:**
- Very similar, but *as* can sometimes sound **more formal** or even **literary**.
- *Since* is more common in everyday speech.

---

### 4. **Now that** — "Because a new situation exists"

**Use it when:** The reason is a **new or changed situation** that makes something possible or different now.

**Examples:**
- **Now that everyone is here**, we can begin.
    - (People just arrived—this is new.)
- *She can relax **now that the exam is over**.*
    - (The exam just finished—this is a new situation.)

**Key difference:** It always implies a **change** from before. You cannot use it for general, unchanging reasons:
- ❌ *Now that the sun is hot, I wear sunscreen every day.* (The sun is always hot—this isn't a "new" situation.)
- ✅ *Now that summer has come, I wear sunscreen every day.* (Summer is a new change.)

---

### 5. **Seeing that / Seeing as** — Informal "Considering that"

**Use it when:** You're giving a reason based on **obvious evidence** or **what you can observe**. Very common in spoken British English.

**Examples:**
- **Seeing that you're already here**, why not join us?
- *I won't go out **seeing as it's raining**.*

**Position:** Usually before the main clause.

**Note:** This is **informal**—don't use it in academic or business writing.

---

### 6. **In that** — Very Formal, Explains "In What Way"

**Use it when:** You're not giving a direct cause, but rather **explaining or clarifying** a statement—*"in what specific sense"* something is true.

**Examples:**
- *This plan is better **in that it costs less**.*
    - (It's not saying *the cost is the cause of being better*—it's saying *the way it's better is in terms of cost*.)
- *She is lucky **in that she has a supportive family**.*

**Position:** Always comes **after** the main clause.

**Key difference:** It explains **how** something is true, not **why**. Very academic/formal.

---

### 7. **For** — Literary, Adds an Explanation (Not a Cause)

**Use it when:** You're adding a **reason or explanation** as an afterthought. It's like *because* but **weaker** and always comes **after** the main clause with a comma. It's mostly used in formal writing or literature.

**Examples:**
- *I decided to stay, **for it was too late to leave**.*
    - (The main point is *I decided to stay*; the reason is extra info.)
- *He must be asleep, **for the lights are off**.*

**Position:** Always **after** the main clause, with a comma before *for*.

**Key difference from *because*:**
- **Because** = direct cause (the reason *why*)
- **For** = an explanation added afterward (not the direct answer to *why*)

⚠️ Never start a sentence with *for* in modern English (unless you're writing poetry).

## Comparison Table — Side by Side

| Conjunction | Use When | Formality | Position | Example |
|-------------|----------|-----------|----------|---------|
| **because** | Direct, strongest reason | Neutral | Before or after | *I left because I was tired.* |
| **since** | Reason is already known | Formal | Usually before | *Since you're here, let's eat.* |
| **as** | Background reason, very similar to *since* | More formal | Usually before | *As I was sick, I stayed home.* |
| **now that** | New/changed situation | Neutral | Usually before | *Now that it's sunny, let's go out.* |
| **seeing that/as** | Obvious, observable reason | Informal | Usually before | *Seeing as you're here, join us.* |
| **in that** | Explains "in what way" | Very formal | After | *It's useful in that it saves time.* |
| **for** | Adds an afterthought explanation | Literary/formal | After (with comma) | *I stayed, for it was late.* |

## Quick Decision Guide — Which One Should You Use?

| Situation | Best Choice |
|-----------|-------------|
| You're answering "Why?" directly | **because** |
| Both you and the listener already know the reason | **since** or **as** |
| A new situation has just happened | **now that** |
| You're speaking informally and giving an obvious reason | **seeing as** |
| You're writing an academic paper and explaining "how" something is true | **in that** |
| You're writing literature and adding an extra explanation | **for** |

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

| ❌ Wrong | ✅ Correct | Why |
|----------|-----------|-----|
| *Since I was tired, I didn't go.* (answering a "Why?" question) | *Because I was tired...* | Use *because* for direct answers to "Why?" |
| *Now that I am human, I eat food.* | *Because I am human, I eat food.* | Being human isn't a "new" situation—it's a general fact. |
| *She left for she was bored.* (in everyday speech) | *She left because she was bored.* | *For* is too formal/literary for casual speech. |
| *I was late as I missed the bus.* | *I was late because I missed the bus.* | If you're stating the main, direct cause, use *because*—*as* sounds too weak here. |
| *In that I was tired, I left.* | *Because I was tired, I left.* | *In that* doesn't mean "because"—it clarifies, not gives a cause. |

---

## Summary Rule of Thumb

> **If you're giving the direct answer to "Why?" → use *because*.**  
> **If the reason is already obvious or background info → use *since* or *as*.**  
> **If something has just changed → use *now that*.**  
> **If you're explaining *how* something is true → use *in that*.**  
> **If you're writing literature → use *for* (carefully).**
