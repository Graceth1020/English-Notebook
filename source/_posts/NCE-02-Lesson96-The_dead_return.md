## ordinary version:

The Japanese festival is famous for it is held for the dead who are said to return to their homes. This festival is held once a year and I went to Japan to experience it myself. Before the festival comes, people will make preparations. They cook food laid out for the dead who are expected to be hungry after a long journey. Special lanterns are made to be hung out each house to help the dead to find their way. On that day, people were dancing and singing on the street to welcome their died relatives or friends. It was my pleasure to join them. The following morning, people got up early to throw the food away into a river or into the sea. The town I lived is near the sea and local residents placed the lanterns on the sea wishing them guild the dead on their return journey to the other world. People standing on the shore watched the lanterns from the shore until they disappeared. As soon as the sun rose, the lanterns could be seen no more.

## Mistakes Identified:

| Original | Problem | Correction |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| is famous for it is held | Wrong structure – "for" should be followed by a noun/gerund, not a clause; use "because" or "for being" | is famous because it is held |
| Before the festival comes | Tense issue – narrative uses past tense later, but "comes" is present – should be "came" or keep as historical present if consistent, but better to use past for narration | Before the festival came |
| people will make preparations | Tense inconsistency – "will" is future; story is past – should be "made" or "would make" | people would make preparations |
| They cook food laid out | Tense issue – "cook" is present; should be "cooked" for past narration | They cooked food laid out |
| to be hung out each house | Missing preposition – "to be hung out **of** each house" or "outside each house" | to be hung outside each house |
| to help the dead to find | "Help" can take "to" or bare infinitive; "to help the dead find" is more natural | to help the dead find |
| were dancing and singing | Correct – consistent with past narration | (no change) |
| their died relatives | Wrong adjective – "died" is a verb; "dead" or "deceased" is the adjective | their dead relatives |
| throw the food away into a river | "Away into" is redundant – "throw the food into" is clearer | throw the food into |
| The town I lived is near the sea | Tense issue – "lived" (past) but "is" (present) – should be "was" | The town I lived in was near the sea |
| wishing them guild the dead | (1) "Guild" is a noun – should be "guide" (verb). (2) Wording is awkward – "wishing them to guide" is better | wishing them to guide the dead |
| from the shore until they disappeared | "From the shore" is repeated – "watched the lanterns until they disappeared" is sufficient | watched the lanterns until they disappeared |

## Corrected Version (changes in bold):

The Japanese festival is famous **because it is** held for the dead who are said to return to their homes. This festival is held once a year, and I went to Japan to experience it myself. Before the festival **came**, people **would make** preparations. They **cooked** food laid out for the dead, who are expected to be hungry after a long journey. Special lanterns are made to be hung **outside** each house to help the dead **find** their way. On that day, people were dancing and singing on the street to welcome their **dead** relatives or friends. It was my pleasure to join them. The following morning, people got up early to **throw** the food **into** a river or into the sea. The town I **lived in** was near the sea, and local residents placed the lanterns on the sea, **wishing them to guide** the dead on their return journey to the other world. People standing on the shore watched the lanterns **until** they disappeared. As soon as the sun rose, the lanterns could be seen no more.

## Additional Minor Improvements (not strictly mistakes, but more natural):

The Japanese **Obon** festival is famous **because** it is held **in honor of** the dead, who are said to return to their **families'** homes. This festival is held once a year, and **I had the chance to** experience it myself **in Japan**. Before the festival **began**, people **would start** preparations. **They** cooked food **and laid** it out for the dead, who were expected to be hungry after a long journey. Special lanterns **were** hung outside each house to help the dead **find their** way. On **the main** day, people **danced** and sang in the streets to welcome their **departed** relatives and friends. It was my pleasure to join them. The following morning, people got up early to **throw leftover** food into a river or the sea. **Since** the town I **stayed in** was near the sea, local residents **floated** lanterns on the water, **hoping they would guide** the dead on their return journey to the other world. People **gathered** on the shore and watched the lanterns until they **faded from sight**. As soon as the sun rose, the lanterns **were no longer** visible.
