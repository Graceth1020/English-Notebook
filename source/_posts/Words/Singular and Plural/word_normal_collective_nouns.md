### Part 1: The Golden Rule (and the Exception)

In **American English**, the rule is strict:
> **A collective noun is usually treated as SINGULAR** because it represents the group as one unit.

- **Singular Verb:** The team **plays** well. (One unit)
- **Singular Verb:** The family **lives** here. (One household)

**The Exception (British English & Specific Cases):**
If the members of the group are acting **individually** and not as a unit, use a **plural verb**.

- **Plural Verb:** The team **are** arguing among themselves. (They are not playing together; they are fighting individually).

**💡 Pro Tip:** To be safe in American English, if the members are acting individually, it is better to say **"The members of the team are arguing."**

### Part 3: How to Choose the Correct Verb

Ask yourself one question: **Are they doing the same thing together, or are they doing different things separately?**

| Collective Noun | Verb | Example | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Jury** | **Singular** | The jury **has** reached a verdict. | They all agreed together. |
| **Jury** | **Plural** | The jury **have** different opinions. | They disagree with each other. |
| **Staff** | **Singular** | The staff **meets** every Monday. | They meet as a single group. |
| **Staff** | **Plural** | The staff **are** taking their vacations. | Each person is choosing different dates. |
| **Audience**| **Singular** | The audience **gives** a standing ovation. | They all stand up together. |
| **Audience**| **Plural** | The audience **were** taking their seats. | They are sitting down one by one. |
