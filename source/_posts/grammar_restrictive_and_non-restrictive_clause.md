This is a very important distinction in English grammar. Let me break it down with **clear rules** and **visual examples**.

---

## The Core Difference in One Sentence

| | Restrictive Clause | Non-Restrictive Clause |
|---|---|---|
| **Purpose** | **Identifies** which one | **Adds extra info** |
| **Commas?** | ❌ No commas | ✅ Yes, commas |
| **Can you remove it?** | ❌ No — sentence changes meaning | ✅ Yes — sentence still makes sense |
| **That vs Which** | Use **that** (or who/which) | Use **which** (or who) — never "that" |

---

## 1. Restrictive Clause (限定性定语从句)

**Definition:** It **restricts** or **identifies** which specific person/thing we're talking about. Without it, you don't know which one.

**Example:**
> *The students **who study hard** will pass the exam.*

- If you remove *"who study hard,"* you get: *The students will pass the exam.*
- Now it means **all** students will pass — which changes the meaning completely!
- So the clause is **necessary** to identify *which* students.

**More examples:**
- *The book **that I borrowed** is on the table.* (Which book? The one I borrowed.)
- *People **who smoke** should quit.* (Which people? Smokers.)

---

## 2. Non-Restrictive Clause (非限定性定语从句)

**Definition:** It gives **extra, optional information** about someone/something already clearly identified. You can remove it and the sentence still makes perfect sense.

**Example:**
> *My brother, **who lives in London**, is a doctor.*

- If you remove *"who lives in London,"* you get: *My brother is a doctor.*
- You already know *which* brother (I only have one, or it's clear from context).
- The clause just adds **bonus info** — it's not needed to identify him.

**More examples:**
- *This car, **which I bought last year**, is very fast.* (You already know which car — this one.)
- *My mother, **who is 70**, still works.* (You already know who my mother is.)

---

## Quick Comparison Table

| | Restrictive | Non-Restrictive |
|---|---|---|
| Commas | No commas | **Commas** around it |
| Can remove? | No — meaning changes | Yes — meaning stays clear |
| Relative pronoun | **that** / who / which | **which** / who (never *that*) |
| Example | *The cake **that I made** is delicious.* (Which cake? Mine.) | *This cake, **which I made**, is delicious.* (Already know which cake.) |

---

## The Famous "That vs Which" Rule

- **Restrictive** → use **that** (or who for people)
    - *The house **that** has a red door is mine.*
- **Non-restrictive** → use **which** (or who for people) — **never "that"**
    - *My house, **which** has a red door, is on this street.*

---

## Why Does This Matter?

Using the wrong one can **change your meaning** or confuse the reader.

**Compare:**
> *The employees **who are lazy** will be fired.*  
> (Only lazy employees get fired — others are safe.)

> *The employees, **who are lazy**, will be fired.*  
> (All employees are lazy, and all will be fired — very different!)

---

## Punctuation Tip (Very Important!)

- **Restrictive:** No commas → information is glued to the noun.
- **Non-restrictive:** Commas → information is "extra" and can be removed.

Think of it this way:
- **Restrictive** = tight hug (no space)
- **Non-restrictive** = gentle pat on the back (with commas as space)
