## ordinary version:

Sometime ago, a light aeroplane with a heavy cargo flew from England to Switzerland. While flying over a mountain, the plane encountered a sudden storm with high winds. The wind was so strong that it was difficult for the pilot to control the plane. Although the pilot has made the plane stable as possible as he could, the plane was crashed into a patch of snow. Fortunately, the pilot was unhurt, but the plane was not able to fly. He roped the plane to a rock to prevent it from sweeping away by wind. Finding a tent in the cargo, he put it up and got into it. He spent the whole night in the tent. Going without food, he slept hungry. Early next morning, he found that the plane had been swept away by the wind and was smashed into pieces, only leaving the cargo and wreckage in the snow.

## Mistakes Identified:

| Original | Problem | Correction |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| Sometime ago | Wrong word – "Sometime" means an unspecified point in time; "Some time ago" (with space) means "a while ago" | Some time ago |
| has made | Tense error – story is in past tense, but "has made" is present perfect; should be past perfect "had made" | had made |
| as possible as he could | Wrong order – correct phrase is "as stable as he could" or "as possible" – mixing both is incorrect | as stable as he could |
| was crashed | "Crashed" can be intransitive – "the plane crashed" is correct; passive "was crashed" is unnecessary | crashed |
| was not able to fly | Correct, but "could not fly" is more natural | could not fly |
| to prevent it from sweeping away | Missing "being" – "from being swept away" is correct passive form | from being swept away |
| by wind | Missing article – "by the wind" | by the wind |
| Finding a tent in the cargo | "In the cargo" is odd – "among the cargo" or "in the cargo hold" | among the cargo |
| he put it up and got into it | Correct | (no change) |
| Going without food, he slept hungry | "Slept hungry" is understandable but not standard; "slept hungry" can be used poetically, but "went to sleep hungry" is better | he went to sleep hungry |
| only leaving the cargo and wreckage | "Only leaving" is fine, but "leaving only" is more natural | leaving only |

## Corrected Version (changes in bold):

**Some time** ago, a light aeroplane with a heavy cargo flew from England to Switzerland. While flying over a mountain, the plane encountered a sudden storm with high winds. The wind was so strong that it was difficult for the pilot to control the plane. Although the pilot **had made** the plane **as stable as he could**, the plane **crashed** into a patch of snow. Fortunately, the pilot was unhurt, but the plane **could not fly**. He roped the plane to a rock to prevent it from **being swept away** by **the** wind. **Finding a tent among** the cargo, he put it up and got into it. He spent the whole night in the tent. Going without food, **he went to sleep hungry**. Early next morning, he found that the plane had been swept away by the wind and was smashed into pieces, **leaving only** the cargo and wreckage in the snow.

## Additional Minor Improvements (not strictly mistakes, but more natural):

**Some time** ago, a light aeroplane **carrying** heavy cargo flew from England to Switzerland. While flying over a mountain, the plane encountered a sudden storm with high winds. The wind was so strong that the pilot could barely control the plane. Although **he tried to keep** the plane **as stable as possible**, it **crashed** into a patch of snow. Fortunately, the pilot was unhurt, but the plane **was no longer flyable**. He **tied** the plane to a rock to prevent it from **being swept away** by the wind. **He found** a tent **among** the cargo, **set it up**, and **climbed inside**. He spent the whole night in the tent. **Without food, he went to sleep hungry**. Early the next morning, he **saw** that the plane had been swept away by the wind and **smashed** into pieces, **leaving only** the cargo and wreckage in the snow.
